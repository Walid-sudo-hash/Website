Planet Guides

Astral meanings, physical compositions, and celestial wonders of our solar system and beyond.

Planets of the Solar System

Mercury

Mercury is a rocky, iron-rich world closest to the Sun. It’s a planet of extremes—blistering heat by day, freezing cold by night. In astrology, Mercury rules communication, intellect, and agility To survive Stick to the twilight zone. Only the border between day and night offers a narrow band where temperatures aren’t instantly deadly. Timing and mobility are your lifelines.

.

Venus

Shrouded in thick clouds of sulfuric acid, Venus is a volcanic inferno with a crushing atmosphere. Astrologically, she governs love, beauty, and attraction, but beneath her charm lies danger. Survival Tip: Rise above the chaos. Float at 50 kilometers above the surface, where temperatures and pressure resemble Earth. Just make sure your ship is acid-proof.

Earth

Our home, rich in life and diversity. Earth is a balanced sphere of air, water, rock, and spirit. It symbolizes grounding, growth, and harmony in astrology. Survival Tip: Protect what sustains you. Earth's ecosystems are fragile. Your survival depends on keeping its atmosphere clean and its resources flowing.

Mars

Rust-colored and cold, Mars is the planet of war and drive. It whispers promises of colonization with its ancient riverbeds and dusty plains. Survival Tip: Bring warmth and shelter. Thin air and subzero temperatures require a pressurized habitat and a reliable heat source. Solar panels and ingenuity are key.

Jupiter

Jupiter, the gas giant, is a swirling storm of hydrogen, helium, and mystery. In astrology, he represents expansion, luck, and wisdom. Survival Tip: Don’t land. Ever. You can’t survive on Jupiter’s surface—it doesn’t have one. But you might orbit safely in a radiation-shielded station, observing the storms from afar.

Saturn

, Majestic and disciplined, Saturn’s icy rings and muted hues reflect his association with karma, structure, and time. Survival Tip: Avoid the rings. Their beauty hides danger—ice and rock moving at breakneck speed. Keep your ship in a safe orbital path.

Uranus

Tilted on its side, Uranus spins like a rolling marble. It’s a cold world of methane clouds and mystery. Astrologically, it rules innovation and disruption. Survival Tip: Brace for magnetic chaos. Its magnetic field is unpredictable. Navigation and shielding systems must adapt to rapid, tilted changes.

Neptune

deep blue ice giant, Neptune is a realm of storms and hidden currents. It’s linked to dreams, illusions, and the subconscious. Survival Tip: Watch for wind. Neptune’s winds are the fastest in the solar system—up to 1,500 mph. Anchor any floating platform with extreme precision.

Pluto (Dwarf Planet)

Tiny and distant, Pluto is icy, dark, and transformative. In astrology, it represents death, rebirth, and the underworld. Survival Tip: Use nuclear warmth. With sunlight too faint to sustain you, a radioactive heat source (like RTGs) is your best chance at surviving the frozen stillness.

Celestial Phenomena

Black Holes

Regions of spacetime with gravitational pulls so strong that nothing can escape. Spiritually, they symbolize the unknown, death, and deep transformation.

Supernovas

A supernova is the colossal explosion of a star. Scientists have identified several types of supernova. One type, called a “core-collapse” supernova, occurs in the last stage in the life of massive stars that are at least eight times larger than our Sun.

Hypernovas

hypernova is a very energetic supernova which is believed to result from an extreme core collapse scenario. In this case, a massive star (>30 solar masses) collapses to form a rotating black hole emitting twin astrophysical jets and surrounded by an accretion disk. It is a type of stellar explosion that ejects material with an unusually high kinetic energy, an order of magnitude higher than most supernovae, with a luminosity at least 10 times greater. Hypernovae release such intense gamma rays that they often appear similar to a type Ic supernova, but with unusually broad spectral lines indicating an extremely high expansion velocity.

Rings of Saturn (NEW)

Saturn's rings are one of the most iconic and spectacular features in our solar system. They are vast, bright bands encircling the planet, extending up to 282,000 km (175,000 miles) wide but incredibly thin—only about 10 meters (33 feet) thick in most places. The rings are divided into several main parts named alphabetically (A, B, C, D, E, F, and G rings), with the A and B rings being the most prominent and dense.

Comets

**Comets** are icy space objects made of dust, rock, and frozen gases. When they get close to the Sun, they heat up and release gas and dust, forming a glowing **coma** and often a **tail**. Comets come from the **Kuiper Belt** or the **Oort Cloud**, and they orbit the Sun in long, oval paths. They become visible when they enter the inner solar system.

Exoplanets

Kepler-452b

Known as "Earth's cousin", this exoplanet orbits within the habitable zone of its star, Kepler-452, and has similar conditions to Earth. It's a prime target for the search for extraterrestrial life.

Kepler-22b

Kepler-22b is one of the first known exoplanets found in the habitable zone of its star. It could possibly have oceans or an atmosphere conducive to life.

Proxima Centauri b

Located in the habitable zone of the closest star to our Sun, Proxima Centauri b has a great potential for life. Its conditions are similar to those of Earth, though it may be subject to intense radiation.

TRAPPIST-1e

One of the seven Earth-like planets orbiting the star TRAPPIST-1, TRAPPIST-1e is in the habitable zone and may have conditions suitable for liquid water.
